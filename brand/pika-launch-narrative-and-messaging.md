# Pika launch messaging kit

## Canonical brand core

**Product name:** Pika

**Positioning statement:**  
Pika helps groups stop circling and pick something—fast. It turns messy “what should we do?” threads into quick, clear decisions people can actually make together.

**Audience promise:**  
When a plan is stuck in the group chat swamp, Pika gives people a quicker, lighter way to land on something everyone can see and respond to—without appointing a planner, starting a spreadsheet, or sending twelve “I’m good with whatever” texts.

**Single-minded takeaway:**  
Pika helps groups stop circling and actually choose something.

**Brand feel:**  
Witty, warm, specific, lightly exasperated about group indecision, and always on the side of momentum.

---

## Usage note

This document maps approved launch copy to specific surfaces and states.  
Where product mechanics are not yet confirmed, copy has been kept neutral and launch-safe.  
Do not reintroduce specific promises about ranking, priority, rewards, access order, referrals, or share outcomes unless those mechanics are explicitly defined in product.

**Undefined mechanics intentionally not referenced:**  
This kit intentionally avoids mentioning queue position, waitlist movement, reward logic, friend-joined effects, referral benefits, milestone unlocks, access acceleration, or any guaranteed result from sharing.

# Surface-by-surface messaging handoff

## 1) Landing page

### Purpose
Introduce Pika clearly, make the problem instantly recognizable, and drive waitlist signups.

### Hero

**Headline:**  
Group decisions, minus the group spiral.

**Subhead:**  
Pika helps friends and groups stop looping in the chat and actually pick something—fast.

**Primary CTA:**  
Join the waitlist

**Secondary CTA:**  
See how it works

**Support line:**  
For dinners, trips, hangs, meetups, and every plan that somehow becomes a 47-message stalemate.

### Problem section

**Headline:**  
The group chat was not built for decisions.

**Body:**  
It is excellent at memes, side quests, and six overlapping opinions. It is much worse at helping five people choose one thing and feel good about it.

**Bullets:**
- One person keeps nudging
- Half the replies are unclear
- New options appear on message 29
- The energy quietly dies before a choice is made

### Value section

**Headline:**  
Pika keeps plans moving.

**Body:**  
Instead of burying preferences inside a long thread, Pika makes the decision legible. That means less chasing, less ambiguity, and a faster path to “we’re doing this.”

**Value points:**
- Clearer group input
- Faster decisions
- Less planning fatigue
- More actual follow-through

### How it works section

**Headline:**  
From “what should we do?” to “cool, done.”

**Body:**  
Pika gives groups a simpler way to throw out options, react clearly, and move toward a choice without the usual planning fog.

**Step 1:**  
**Start with the question**  
Drop in the decision your group keeps circling.

**Step 2:**  
**Let people weigh in fast**  
Get quick, visible responses instead of ten versions of “either works.”

**Step 3:**  
**Actually land on a plan**  
See what’s rising, stop the rehash, and move.

### Social proof / relatability section

**Headline:**  
If you’ve ever sent “any preferences?” into the void, this is for you.

**Body:**  
Pika is built for the friend who starts the plan, the roommate who just wants a decision, and the whole group that would love to stop pretending “I’m fine with anything” is helpful.

### FAQ

**What is Pika?**  
Pika is a tool for helping groups make decisions faster, especially when a chat starts getting messy or indecisive.

**Who is it for?**  
Friends, roommates, casual groups, and anyone who regularly tries to coordinate plans with other people.

**Is this only for big events?**  
No. Pika is especially useful for lightweight plans—the kinds of decisions that should be easy but somehow never are.

**Why join the waitlist now?**  
You’ll hear when Pika launches and get updates as we get closer.

## 7) Dependencies / undefined mechanics note

### Confirmed-safe claims
These are approved and safe to use across launch surfaces:
- Pika helps groups decide faster
- Pika helps stop the group chat spiral
- Pika turns messy planning threads into clearer decisions
- Pika is for friends, roommates, and small groups making everyday plans
- Users can join a waitlist and receive launch updates

### Undefined or not-yet-confirmed mechanics
Do not write copy that assumes any of the following unless product confirms them:
- Waitlist ranking
- Queue position
- Priority access
- Referral rewards
- Milestone unlocks
- Friend-joined effects
- Exclusive access tiers
- Guaranteed outcomes from sharing
- Defined post-share state changes
- Any required follow-up flow after invite receipt

### Replacement guidance
If a flow needs copy before mechanics are finalized:
- Say “share with friends” instead of “earn rewards”
- Say “join the waitlist” instead of “claim your spot before it rises”
- Say “get launch updates” instead of “get priority access”
- Say “your friend invited you” instead of any copy that implies benefit or status change

### Escalation note
If product later defines referral or waitlist logic, this document should be revised with a mechanics-specific layer rather than patching promotional claims into existing surfaces ad hoc.

## Voice guardrails

### Keep
- Specific observations about real group behavior
- Warm humor rooted in recognition
- Plainspoken benefits
- Human, conversational phrasing
- Momentum-oriented language

### Avoid
- Corporate abstraction
- Productivity jargon
- Hypey startup language
- Vague “community” claims
- Any promise the product has not clearly earned

### Preferred vocabulary
- decide
- pick
- plan
- group chat
- circling
- stuck
- move
- option
- crew
- thread
- momentum

### Avoid vocabulary
- seamless
- empower
- optimize
- frictionless
- leverage
- transform
- robust
- synergy
- supercharge

---

## Creative north star

Pika should always sound like it understands the deeply familiar absurdity of trying to make one simple plan with several other people.  
The voice is social, observant, and useful—not managerial, not breathless, and never generic.
