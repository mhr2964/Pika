# Overview

This pack assembles the current growth launch-readiness work for Pika into one artifact: the launch funnel backbone, waitlist mechanics, first-touch conversion copy, and the share-loop framework.

The growth approach is intentionally product-led. It leans on expressive planning outputs, friend-group coordination, and socially legible results people naturally want to share. It avoids spammy referrals, generic giveaway tactics, and broad “join our newsletter” framing.

The working wedge throughout is: Pika helps friend groups go from vague travel/plan talk to one clear, shared direction.

# Launch Funnel

## Funnel shape

1. **First touch**
   - User encounters a homepage, shared result card, shared planning board, or invite from a friend.
   - Messaging centers on reducing friend-group planning chaos and making preferences visible quickly.

2. **Landing-page conversion**
   - Visitor sees one of the conversion copy variants.
   - Primary CTA is waitlist join / early access request.
   - Secondary reinforcement clarifies the product outcome: clearer group decisions, less back-and-forth, more momentum.

3. **Waitlist capture**
   - Minimal-friction form capture.
   - Collect core identity/contact plus lightweight context on planning intent.
   - Immediately set expectation for what happens next.

4. **Post-submit momentum**
   - User receives an on-page confirmation state with clear next steps.
   - Prompt for low-friction sharing tied to actual utility: invite tripmates, share taste/result artifact, or reserve a group spot.

5. **Referral / group expansion**
   - Growth comes from collaborative planning behavior:
     - “bring your group in”
     - “share your result”
     - “start with the people you actually plan with”
   - Social proof accrues through multi-person planning entry, not abstract points-chasing.

6. **Activation-ready cohort formation**
   - Waitlist is organized into launch-relevant buckets:
     - active trip planners
     - friend-group organizers
     - socially expressive sharers
     - couples / roommates / friend groups with imminent planning needs
   - Early access can then be granted to groups most likely to create visible planning artifacts and sharing loops.

## Funnel principles

- Every step should answer: “What useful thing do I get or unlock?”
- Sharing should be framed as improving the plan, not marketing the product.
- The best referral is a real co-planner entering the product context.
- Copy should emphasize clarity, group alignment, and specific outcomes over “AI magic.”

# Waitlist Mechanics

## Core waitlist structure

## Primary goal
Capture high-intent early users and organize them into cohorts most likely to activate with real group-planning behavior.

## Recommended fields
- First name
- Email
- Who they plan with
  - friends
  - partner
  - roommates
  - family
- What they’re planning
  - trip
  - weekend
  - birthday
  - group outing
  - “something else”
- Timing / urgency
  - planning now
  - planning this month
  - just curious
- Optional free-response:
  - “What kind of plan do you wish was easier to make with your group?”

## Submission flow
After form submission, show:
- success confirmation
- what early access means
- lightweight nudge to invite the people they actually plan with
- optional share action anchored in usefulness, not rewards language

Example confirmation framing:
- “You’re on the list.”
- “Pika is built for plans that involve other people — if you already know who you’d bring, invite them now so you can start together.”

## Ranking / prioritization logic
Prioritize users who indicate:
- active planning intent
- imminent timing
- multi-person planning contexts
- strong pain around coordination
- high likelihood of bringing in additional collaborators

Do **not** over-index on vanity referrals alone. Favor users whose entries imply real plan formation and natural sharing opportunities.

## Waitlist segmentation
Suggested segments:
- **Immediate planners** — planning something now or this month
- **Group organizers** — often the person who coordinates
- **Social sharers** — likely to share a result card or planning artifact
- **High-friction groups** — friend groups with decision paralysis
- **Potential anchor users** — likely to bring 2–5 real collaborators

## Referral mechanic
Use a soft, utility-first mechanism:
- invite people you actually plan with
- reserve early group access
- bring your trip crew / planning group together

Avoid:
- cash-like incentives
- leaderboard-heavy dynamics
- abstract referral contests disconnected from planning value

## Suggested post-submit states

### State A — solo submitter
“Thanks — you’re in line for early access. If there’s a group you already plan with, invite them now so you can start with actual preferences instead of another chaotic group chat.”

### State B — active planner
“You’re exactly who Pika is for. Want better suggestions faster when access opens? Add the people you’re planning with.”

### State C — socially expressive user
“If your group always debates where to go, Pika works best when everyone drops in their taste. Share your invite link with the people who’d actually weigh in.”

## Success metrics
- landing page → waitlist conversion
- waitlist submit → share/invite action
- average number of real collaborators invited per submitter
- percentage of waitlist signups tied to active planning intent
- cohort activation rate once invited

# Share-Loop Framework

## 1) Result Card Share Loop
- **loop name:** Result Card Share Loop
- **user moment:** A user gets a funny, accurate, or unexpectedly specific planning/taste result they want to show someone immediately.
- **trigger:** Right after result generation, when the output feels identity-revealing, socially legible, or usefully specific.
- **share object:** A compact result card with:
  - punchy title
  - one-line explanation
  - tasteful visual identity
  - CTA back into Pika
- **why someone shares it:** It says something recognizable about their travel/planning style and invites reactions from friends.
- **why recipient clicks:** They want to see the full result, compare themselves, or use it to start a group plan.
- **ideal destination:** A landing page that:
  - explains what the result means
  - invites the recipient to get their own
  - gives a path into planning with others
- **loop payoff:** One result creates both self-expression and a natural “you should try yours” interaction.

## 2) Group Invite Loop
- **loop name:** Group Invite Loop
- **user moment:** One person realizes the output improves when more of the real group participates.
- **trigger:** After an initial solo plan, preference capture, or recommendation draft.
- **share object:** Direct invite link framed around collaboration.
- **why someone shares it:** They need actual input from the people involved, not more guessing.
- **why recipient clicks:** They’ve been pulled into a real plan that concerns them.
- **ideal destination:** A join flow with immediate context:
  - what plan they’re joining
  - who invited them
  - what input is needed from them
- **loop payoff:** Each plan naturally recruits the participants required to make the plan better.

## 3) Planning Snapshot Loop
- **loop name:** Planning Snapshot Loop
- **user moment:** A user reaches a satisfying intermediate artifact worth showing: top options, shared vibe, narrowed destination set, or group alignment snapshot.
- **trigger:** After enough inputs exist to produce a “this is getting real” planning snapshot.
- **share object:** A concise planning visual summarizing where the group stands.
- **why someone shares it:** It reduces repetitive explanation and gives the group something concrete to react to.
- **why recipient clicks:** They want to see the full plan status, vote, or understand what’s emerging.
- **ideal destination:** A plan page that preserves context and prompts the next useful action.
- **loop payoff:** Progress itself becomes shareable, which pulls more people into the planning process.

## 4) Compare-Yourself Loop
- **loop name:** Compare-Yourself Loop
- **user moment:** A user sees a profile/result that begs comparison with a friend, partner, or group.
- **trigger:** Once the product can say something like:
  - “you’re the spontaneous one”
  - “your group wants comfort with one wildcard night”
  - “you and your friend overlap here, clash here”
- **share object:** A comparison-ready result or prompt.
- **why someone shares it:** Comparison is playful, socially sticky, and useful for planning compatibility.
- **why recipient clicks:** They want to see their own profile and how they match up.
- **ideal destination:** A personalized entry that leads quickly to their own result and then into group comparison.
- **loop payoff:** Identity + contrast creates a repeatable friend-to-friend acquisition path.

## 5) Decision Unlock Loop
- **loop name:** Decision Unlock Loop
- **user moment:** A group is stuck, and one person shares Pika as the mechanism to finally resolve the decision.
- **trigger:** High-friction indecision in chats or ongoing planning threads.
- **share object:** An invite or planning prompt positioned as the fastest way to break deadlock.
- **why someone shares it:** They want to stop endless back-and-forth and move the group toward one decision.
- **why recipient clicks:** The problem is immediate and relevant; the tool promises resolution.
- **ideal destination:** A plan-start flow optimized for quick group input and visible convergence.
- **loop payoff:** Existing coordination pain becomes the acquisition engine.

## Framework-wide guidance
- Shareables should feel expressive, useful, and lightweight.
- Each loop should preserve context when clicked.
- The recipient should understand in seconds:
  - what this is
  - why they got it
  - what they can do next
- The strongest loops are tied to real planning progress, not empty virality.
