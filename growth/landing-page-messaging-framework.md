# Pika landing-page messaging framework
_Status: near-launch acquisition pack / canonical messaging framework_

## Purpose
Give Pika a landing page that feels immediately legible to people who already coordinate social plans in group chats, without over-claiming product breadth. The page should convert visitors who recognize the pain of messy group coordination and want a clearer way to get everyone on the same plan.

## Positioning guardrails
- Anchor on **social group planning**, not general productivity.
- Speak to **friend groups, roommates, birthday organizers, trip planners, hobby circles, and socially active group admins**.
- Emphasize **clarity from chat chaos** and **a shared plan everyone can follow**.
- Avoid implying full event-management, calendar replacement, workplace scheduling, or enterprise collaboration.
- Near-launch tone: believable, useful, lightweight, and social.

---

## 1) Hero framework

### Hero objective
Make the right visitor say: “Yes, this is exactly the problem in my group chat.”

### Primary headline options
Use one canonical option at a time; do not mix metaphors.

**Option A — strongest default**
- **Make group plans clear before they disappear in the chat.**

**Option B**
- **Turn messy group chats into one clear plan.**

**Option C**
- **Stop losing the plan in the group chat.**

### Supporting subheadline
- **Pika helps friends coordinate plans in one shareable place, so choosing a time, place, and next step feels simple instead of chaotic.**

Alternate tighter version:
- **Pika turns scattered messages into a shared plan everyone can see and react to.**

### Hero CTA
Primary:
- **Get early access**

Secondary:
- **See how it works**  
Use only if the page has an anchored product section or visual demo below the fold.

### Hero visual guidance
Show a believable before/after transformation:
- left: noisy group chat fragments
- right: one clean shared plan with the key decision surfaced

The visual should communicate:
- group context
- fewer moving parts
- a visible plan artifact
- lightweight social participation

Avoid:
- abstract dashboards
- dense productivity UI
- workplace-looking task boards
- fake “AI assistant” framing unless already proven in product

## 2) Problem / benefit framing

### Core problem statement
Groups already try to plan in chat, but the useful details get buried. People miss the latest decision, repeat questions, drift, or stop responding because the plan never becomes clear.

### Problem section headline options
- **The plan gets buried.**
- **Group chat is where plans start — and where they get lost.**
- **Everyone’s in the chat, but nobody’s on the same page.**

### Problem bullets
- Messages pile up faster than decisions.
- The latest plan is hard to find.
- People ask the same questions again.
- One person ends up manually summarizing everything.
- Momentum dies before the group decides.

### Benefit framing headline options
- **Pika gives the group one version of the plan.**
- **From chat chaos to a clear shared plan.**
- **Keep everyone aligned without doing organizer overtime.**

### Benefit bullets
- Pull the key details into one place.
- Make the current plan easy to see.
- Help everyone react, align, and move forward.
- Reduce the back-and-forth that slows groups down.
- Make social planning feel lighter, not heavier.

### Tone note
Benefits should feel like relief, not optimization theater. This is about reducing social friction, not “maximizing efficiency.”

---

## 3) How-it-works section

### Section objective
Explain the product in a way that feels real and simple in under 3 steps.

### Section headline
- **How Pika works**

### Recommended 3-step structure

**1. Start with the group conversation**
- Your group is already discussing where to go, when to meet, or what to do.

**2. Pika turns the mess into a shared plan**
- The important details become visible in one place, so people can quickly understand the current plan.

**3. Everyone can follow and react**
- The group sees the same plan, responds around it, and moves toward a decision with less confusion.

### Tighter alternative version
**1. Capture the plan**  
Pull the important details out of the thread.

**2. Share one clear view**  
Give everyone the same place to check what’s happening.

**3. Get the group aligned**  
Help people react and move forward without repeating the whole conversation.

### How-it-works copy constraints
Do not claim:
- automatic perfection
- end-to-end logistics management
- universal integration coverage
- full scheduling replacement

Do claim:
- clearer shared context
- easier coordination
- lower group friction
- better follow-through

## 4) Social-proof placeholder strategy

### Goal
Add trust without inventing traction, inflated numbers, or fake testimonials.

### What is valid now
Use placeholders that can be swapped as real signals come in.

#### Placeholder type A — audience recognition
Header:
- **Built for the kinds of groups who already do this in chat**

Examples:
- Friend-group trip planners
- Birthday organizers
- Roommates coordinating the week
- Hobby groups planning meetups
- The person who always ends up summarizing the plan

This works as “recognition proof” before traditional proof exists.

#### Placeholder type B — product-slice realism
Header:
- **Made for real, everyday social plans**

Examples:
- dinner plans
- weekend trips
- birthdays
- meetups
- casual group outings

This reassures visitors that Pika is designed for actual use cases, not abstract planning.

#### Placeholder type C — founder/use-case quote block
Until real user quotes exist, use a clearly non-testimonial statement:
- **Pika is built for the moment when a group chat has plenty of messages, but still no clear plan.**

Label as:
- “Why we built Pika”
or
- “What Pika is for”

Do **not** format as a fake customer testimonial.

#### Placeholder type D — access framing
- **Early access for socially active groups**
- **Joining early groups testing a better way to coordinate plans**
This creates legitimacy without pretending there is scale.

### What is not valid now
- Fake user counts
- “Loved by…” claims without evidence
- Placeholder avatars posing as customers
- Press logos
- Institutional trust badges unrelated to real adoption

## 5) CTA logic

### Primary page goal
Capture qualified early interest from people who immediately identify with the problem.

### Primary CTA
- **Get early access**

Reason:
- Fits near-launch positioning
- Honest about stage
- Broad enough for both curiosity and intent

### Secondary CTA
Use only if a valid product preview exists:
- **See how it works**
- **View a sample plan**

If no meaningful preview exists, do not add a weak secondary CTA.

### CTA placement logic
**Above the fold**
- One clear primary CTA

**After problem/benefit section**
- Repeat primary CTA for visitors who are now convinced by the pain/solution framing

**After how-it-works**
- Repeat primary CTA with a little more specificity:
  - **Get early access for your group**
  - **Join early access**

**Near footer**
- Final CTA with audience reminder:
  - **Bring clarity to your group plans**
  - button: **Get early access**

### Microcopy near CTA
Optional support lines:
- **For friend groups, trips, dinners, birthdays, and everyday plans**
- **Built for groups who already coordinate in chat**
- **Early access for people who organize plans more often than they’d like**

### CTA anti-patterns
Avoid:
- “Start for free” if access is limited
- “Book a demo”
- “Increase productivity”
- “Join thousands”
- “Never plan the same way again”

## 6) Target audience language

### Primary audience
People coordinating lightweight social plans with others:
- friend-group trip planners
- birthday and dinner organizers
- roommates
- hobby circles planning meetups
- socially active group admins
- the “default organizer” in a group

### Audience section objective
Help visitors self-identify quickly without making the page feel segmented or overbuilt.

### Recommended section headline

### Supporting copy

### Audience bullets
- Planning a birthday dinner
- Organizing a weekend trip
- Coordinating roommates
- Getting a hobby group to commit to a meetup
- Helping indecisive friends land on one plan

### Language to use
- group chat
- shared plan
- clear plan
- friends
- everyone on the same page
- react
- decide
- coordinate
- stop repeating the plan
- keep it moving

### Language to avoid
- project management
- stakeholders
- team workflows
- productivity suite
- optimize coordination layers

- event infrastructure
